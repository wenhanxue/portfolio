Motivation

In lecture, Professor D'Ignazio highlighted the absence of eviction data despite ongoing political demands since 2016. This absence underscores the need for further investigation and attention to this critical issue. According to a report by Homes for All MA, the COVID-19 pandemic exacerbated eviction filing rates in Massachusetts, with many filings intentionally targeting marginalized populations, including Black and Latinx communities, as well as single mothers. The report also emphasized that corporate and absentee property owners are more likely to file evictions. The dataset I am analyzing includes changes in corporate ownership and owner-occupancy rates from 2004 to 2024 across Boston neighborhoods, along with detailed demographic information for each neighborhood. While the dataset does not include specific eviction filing data for Boston, it provides an opportunity to explore which neighborhoods have higher rates of corporate ownership and which demographic groups are most affected. In the absence of eviction data, I aim to examine changes in the racial and ethnic makeup of neighborhoods over time, which may indirectly reflect the consequences of eviction filings and housing instability. Additionally, the Homes for All MA report noted that "Black, Brown, and Indigenous renters are more likely to be evicted than white renters." This raises the question of where Asian Americans and Pacific Islanders (AAPI) stand in this issue, particularly given the diversity in socioeconomic background within this population.

Summary

In Boston, corporate ownership rates have increased significantly since 2004, with a steeper rise recently starting in 2019. This trend is particularly evident in neighborhoods with historically low owner occupancy rates (e.g., South Boston Waterfront, Longwood, Downtown, and Fenway), which allowed for corporate investors to purchase and rent out residential properties. Greater percentage of BIPOC populations reside in neighborhoods with historically high owner occupancy rates, which resulted in lower corporate ownership rates in those neighborhoods. However, this is not contradicting Homes for All MA's report, as BIPOC residents in high corporate ownership neighborhoods may still be more likely to face evictions than the white population. Corporate investors appear to avoid neighborhoods with high proportions of institutional facilities (e.g., West Roxbury, Roslindale, and Hyde Park) and instead target areas with non-institutional group quarters like homeless shelters (e.g., South Boston Waterfront, Downtown, and Allston). This pattern aligns with the observation that higher corporate ownership neighborhoods exhibits higher proportion of homeless shelters, suggesting that corporate ownership may have contributed to rising housing costs and instability for renters in the areas. In terms of race and ethnicity, Indigenous populations have very limited prescence in Boston due to historical oppression and displacement, which suggests the necessity to make sure their experience and needs are not overlooked in housing instability discussions. Lastly, neighborhoods with large Asian American and Pacific Islander (AAPI) populations, such as Chinatown, Allston, and Fenway, have seen significant increases in corporate ownership rates (exacerbated after the pandemic) and corresponding declines in owner occupancy rates, despite them being historically high. This shift suggests growing housing instability for AAPI residents in Boston, especially in recent years.
