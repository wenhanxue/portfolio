Reading & Critique

Insights about the data/visualization:

  • This visualization depicts the number of newly reported COVID-19 cases in the United States (shown in red) from early 2020 to the start of January 2022, when the visualization was published. Based on the red trend line, the earliest reported cases of COVID-19 in the U.S. appeared in March 2020, suggesting that this was when the pandemic began to spread widely in the country.
  • The visualization exhibits three distinct peaks in COVID-19 infections in the United States, each corresponding to the emergence of a new variant of the virus. in chronological order, these peaks align with the spread of the original strain of the coronavirus, followed by the Delta variant, and finally the Omicron variant. This pattern indicates that the introduction of these variants was a primary driver of these peaks in cases.
  • Additionally, the visualization shows two notable troughs, both occurring between April and July of 2020 and 2021. According to Dr. Shaman's opinion article, these declines in cases are likely due to higher temperatures during the summer period, which create a less favorable environment for virus transmission. Conversely, two of the peaks occurred at the beginning of 2021 and 2022 coinciding with the winter season. This pattern can be explained by increased travel and social gatherings during the winter holidays like Christmas and New Year that facilitated the spread of coronavirus.

Design Critique:

What does the visualization do well?

  • This visualization does a great job in its creative use of a spiral shape to depict trends as opposed to more conventional linear chart format. By transforming the x-axis into a spiral, the author effectively addresses the constraints posed by the medium on which it is displayed. Given that the visualization was published in an opinion article in The New York Times, it was most likely designed with mobile readers in mind. The spiral format makes sure that the chart fits neatly on a phone screen, avoiding the need to scroll horizontally and preventing the chart from being shrunk to a smaller scale that compromises readability. Additionally, the author seamlessly incorporates direct labeling on the x-axis, marking the years as the spiral progresses, which eliminates the need for readers to constantly refer back to a legend. The arrows also helps guide readers on the direction in which they should follow the trend, which is especially helpful for visualizations with time-based data. Lastly, the visualization has a relatively low data-ink ratio, adhering to minimalistic design philosophy. The use of red for the trend line, gray dotted lines for the months, and black for the x-axis creates a clear visual hierarchy. In particular, the dotted gray lines are an excellent design choice for representing less important details like the months, as they fade into the background and ensures that they do not distract from the primary data and message of the visualization.

Where do we see potential space for improvement and how?

  • While the visualization effectively conveys the trend, it falls short in showing the precise quantities that the readers may find important. This limitation is due to an inherent trade-off of the spiral design: the x-axis is not a continuous, straight line, which makes it difficult for readers to compare the magnitude of two points that are not visually aligned. For instance, readers may not immediately recognize that January 2022 had more covid cases than January 2021, as these points are not positioned side by side. To address this problem, the legend could be improved by (1) including more intermediate values (rather than going from 0 directly to 150k), (2) inserting discrete breaks instead of a continuous scale, and (3) color-coding each value or value range to make changes in value more detectable. However, these adjustments might conflict with the author's choice of a minimalistic aesthetic and low data-ink ratio.
  • Another area for improvement is the lack of visual cues to guide the readers' attention. For example, bolding a small segment of the beginning of the x-axis could help navigate the audience to the starting point of the spiral/trend. Additionally, a larger, more centered title could better contextualize the visualization for readers. Considering that the audience is mainly general public who may not be familiar with reading charts, the absence of a clear title currently may leave some viewers confused. Lastly, this visualization appeared right below the title "Here’s When We Expect Omicron to Peak" in the original article. If this is a key message the visualization aims to convey, annotating where exactly the author expects Omicron to peak (January of 2022, as mentioned in the article) with text labels or a distinct color might make this takeaway more evident to the audience.

Visualization Sketches

Through critiquing Dr. Jeffrey Shaman’s visualization, I once again learned that there is no single perfect design choice or solution—each decision comes with its own set of tradeoffs. My approach to generating these sketches is very much guided by this lesson learned. My goal is to explore diverse visual forms and encoding techniques, experimenting with how they represent the data and convey the intended message, thereby making more informed choices when designing my final visualization. By creating multiple different sketches, I hope to better understand the strengths and weaknesses of different design choices in the context of showcasing the COVID-19 data we are given. I also aim to understand the tradeoffs involved in balancing functionality and clarity (and perhaps, aesthetics).

Design Rationale for Sketch 1:

Purpose and Motivation

  • This sketch mostly follows the original visualization to test the effectiveness of the improvements I suggested in my critique. One of my main goals is to make the actual number of COVID-19 cases more quantifiable while still making the trends over time visible. I also (re)designed this with the general public (as the main audience group) in mind and prioritized ease of understanding for those who may not have extensive experience reading charts.

    • Major Features

  • I added a larger and more descriptive title to the visualization to immediately provide context for readers. I also included annotations of emergence of Delta and Omicron variants, as these information might be of interest and informative to the general public.
  • I modified the original continuous scale by inserting discrete bars that vary in length and color to more precisely represent counts of cases. The bar colors used a gradient from green to red that intuitively shows severity, with green representing low number of cases and red representing high numbers.

    • What Worked Well

  • The modified bars effectively conveys the magnitude of case counts while maintaining the overall trend.
  • The annotations and title provide necessary context that makes this visualization more informative and accessible.

    • What Didn't Work Well and Future Explorations

  • Despite these improvements, the spiral shape may still cause a learning curve for the general public. I showed both the original and redesigned visualizations to my friends with varying levels of experience in social science and reading charts and they all agreed that this shape posed a learning curve on them. Therefore, I will explore more conventional visual forms to improve the accessibility of the visualization, making it more engaging and less cognitively demanding for readers.
  • I am afraid that using weekly averages might downplay the actual impact of the pandemic, and plan to use weekly or monthly sums to better reflect the impact.

    Purpose and Motivation

    • Final Visualization Design

    Design Process & Design Decisions

    There are three major components to the story I am telling in this visualization: (1) The ebb and flow of, as well as the actual impact of pandemic across different phases by depicting the trends for cases and deaths; (2) the influence of the major variants (the original strain, Delta, and Omicron) on the trends for cases and deaths, given that Dr. Shaman expected Omicron to be more infectious but less fatal in the article; and (3) the recurring seasonal trends, such as decreased virus transmission during summer months (due to higher temperatures) and peaks during winter (due to holiday gatherings and subsequent delays in reporting positive cases).

    The visualization uses a small multiples format, with each column representing a single year (2020, 2021, 2022) aligned horizontally. This design allows readers to see the entire trend of the pandemic while also comparing trends within each year and across years. The grid is segmented by the emergence of major variants, with distinct background colors (green for the original strain, pink for Delta, and blue for Omicron) indicating the periods during which each variant was dominant. These segments are also labeled along the x-axis using the same color scheme to provide redundant coding.

    Two neutral yet contrasting colors were chosen: purple for monthly new confirmed cases and orange for monthly new deaths. These color choices were carefully considered to ensure accessibility for individuals with colorblindness. To address the significant disparity in scale between cases and deaths, a zoomed-in version of the grid is included specifically for deaths. This zoomed-in version maintains the same orange color encoding and configuration as the main grid to ensure consistency and minimize the risk of misreading. Additionally, a to-scale representation of the zoomed-in version's y-axis is placed next to the main grid’s y-axis, to serve as an indication of differing scales in hopes of avoiding misreading.

    The dataset used for this visualization reports daily counts of new confirmed cases and deaths from January 2020 to December 2022. However, due to the negative numbers in December 2022 (which, according to metadata, account for overcounting from prior days), it is not included in the visualization. The daily counts were aggregated into monthly sums for each year.

    To make the visualization easier to understand, the visualization includes detailed annotations that explain seasonal patterns, trends, and significant milestones in pandemic, such as the first reported case, the release of COVID-19 vaccines, and comparisons of deaths during the Delta and Omicron waves. These annotations provide informative context and make the visualization more accessible to a general audience. The title offers immediate context, and a concise summary beneath it serves as a TL;DR, summarizing the main takeaways for readers who may lack experience in interpreting complex graphs.

    While the zoomed-in grid for deaths effectively addresses the issue of difference in scale and allows more nuances to be shown, it adds complexity to the visualization. Although a to-scale representation of the zoomed-in y-axis is added in attempts to mitigate the risk of misreading, it does not entirely eliminate this possibility.

    Reflecting retrospectively, the TL;DR section could also be refined to more accurately convey the main takeaways. For instance, explicitly stating the total number of deaths as a key takeaway in the TL;DR would emphasize the pandemic’s profound impact on communities in the U.S. and potentially reduce the likelihood of misinterpretation. The decreased percentage of deaths among vaccinated individuals is added to avoid downplaying the number of deaths caused by covid when highlighting that death rate caused by Omicron is less than that caused by Delta (kindly pointed out by the TA Zoe :)).

It also allowed me to explore different types of visual forms and encoding techniques to learn about the strengths and weaknesses, as well as the tradeoffs in each one in the context of this dataset. This provided me opportunity to think about which ones to use to balance between effectively communicating my data and message and the clarity and accessibility of the visualization.     For instance, I deviated from the weekly averaged used in the original visualization to monthly sums in hopes of more concretely plotting out the impact of COVID-19. As a result, I moved from spiral shape (which better fits a mobile screen) to more conventional and common line charts. There are also successes in my sketches, such as the inclusion of titles and more elaborate annotations to add context and more informative explanations to my visualization for the general audience. Those I found them particularly effective in my sketches, and I continued on using them in my final design.
